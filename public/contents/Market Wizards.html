Stock Selection

Future

  • The market sells on future rather than current earnings. Stocks form bottoms when the current news is terrible and top out when the public is ecstatic about glowing earnings reports, stock splits, and so on.

News

  • Market's Response to Fundamentals: Rather than predicting based on conventional fundamental analysis, the focus is on observing how the market reacts to fundamental information. For example, a market that shrugs off bad news might indicate a strong upcoming move (p. 121, p. 144).

Earnings

  • Earnings Growth: Look for 20-50% quarterly earnings growth, as top-performing stocks often show a 70% earnings increase before significant price moves.
    • Reason: Earnings growth is a reliable indicator of a company's financial health and stock performance.
  • Consistency: Prioritize stocks with steady annual earnings growth and check for deceleration in the last two quarters.
    • Reason: Slowing earnings suggest a company’s momentum is weakening, which could signal an impending price drop.

A+ Ticker in A+ Sector in a good Market

  • Evaluate the overall market trend
    • Even promising-looking stocks are likely to struggle against a negative market trend.
  • Identify which market sectors or groups show the strongest technical performance.
  • Focus specifically on finding the 1-2 best individual stock chart patterns within that strong sector

Bear & Bull Phase

  • Divergence: Watch for divergence between the Dow and the advance/decline line as a bear market signal.
    • Reason: This indicates that fewer stocks are participating in market rallies, which weakens the overall market foundation.
  • Leading Stocks' Decline: When leading stocks drop, it often foreshadows a bear market.
    • Reason: These stocks typically lead the market, so their weakness is a red flag.
  • Price Weakness Indicators: Market weakness can sometimes be seen even during uptrends, indicated by decelerating momentum

Old and Young Market

  • One reason why many types of technical analysis don’t work too well is because such methods are often applied indiscriminately.
    • For example, if you see a head-and-shoulders pattern form in what is the market equivalent of a twenty-year-old, the odds are that the market is not likely to die so quickly. However, if you see the same chart formation in the market equivalent of an eighty-year-old, there’s a much better chance of that pattern being an accurate indicator of a price top.

Price Movement: Technical analysis is the study of price and volume relationships to gain an insight into future trends


Timing

  • I use liquidity considerations and technical analysis for timing. Valuation only tells me how far the market can go once a catalyst enters the picture to change the market direction.
    • Waiting for the Move: It is recommended not to enter a trade until the price move is already underway to avoid tying up capital.

Volume

  • Low Volume at Highs: Weak volume at market highs signals a vulnerable rally.
    • Reason: Low demand suggests there isn’t enough buying power to sustain higher prices.
  • Volume increases with no meaningful price upward progress: potential distribution.
    • Reason: Large traders or institutions may be selling their positions (distributing), while smaller buyers are continuing to purchase, thus holding the price relatively stable. This lack of price movement despite higher volume signals that supply is meeting demand, which typically happens near market peaks.
  • Volume During Consolidation: A drop in volume during consolidation phases is a positive sign, indicating that sellers are drying up and a breakout could occur.
    • Reason: Continued high volume could suggest a top, meaning sellers are still active.
  • High Volume at Bottoms: If volume spikes at market bottoms but prices don’t fall much, it suggests strong buyer support.
    • Reason: High volume combined with price stability implies buyers are stepping in to absorb selling pressure.

Support and Resistance

  • If a prior high for a stock was 65, then they consider 65 to be major resistance, and if, for example, the market rallies to 66, they consider resistance to be broken. This is nonsense. Support and resistance should be considered approximate areas, not precise points.

Fake Break Out

  • Behavioral Patterns of Traders: Many traders place stop-loss orders near significant price levels, like recent highs or lows. These stops are essentially safety nets to limit losses if the market moves against their position. When many stops accumulate around the same levels, this creates a concentrated pocket of liquidity.
  • Awareness by Floor Traders (Locals): Professional traders, especially floor traders or locals who understand these behavioral patterns, are aware of where these stops are likely to be clustered. This knowledge allows them to take advantage of the situation.
  • Triggering Stop Orders: As prices approach these concentrated pockets of stops, locals may attempt to trigger the stops.
  • Temporary Price Extensions: When a large number of stop orders are triggered, this can cause a sharp, short-term price extension. For a moment, the market seems to break out in one direction (above the previous high or below the previous low).
  • Price Reversal After Stops Are Filled: If the move was primarily driven by these local traders' actions, once the stop orders have been executed, the liquidity from those orders is exhausted. The market can then reverse, falling back below the previous high (or rising back above the previous low), making the initial breakout a fake out. Essentially, this signals that the breakout was not driven by real demand or supply, but by the mechanics of stop-hunting.

Risk Management

  • Cut Losses Early: If a stock falls 7% below purchase price, sell without hesitation.
    • Reason: Limiting losses early prevents small losses from turning into major setbacks.
  • When trading large, it is essential that the market go immediately in your favor; otherwise, the position should be pared down quickly. This measure is essential to ensure financial survival when you are wrong in a situation that you thought was highly favorable.
  • Breakouts Re-Entering Base: If a breakout fails and returns to its base, sell at least 50% of your position
    • Reason: Failed breakouts indicate weakening momentum, increasing the risk of further declines.
  • Avoid Chasing: Do not buy stocks already 15-20% above their price base.
    • Reason: Buying too late increases exposure to corrections or reversals.
  • Lesson in Over-leverage: Being right on market direction but wrong on leverage can still result in significant losses, if not even buffer time were prepared until market reacts

Trading Strategy

  • Catching the "Easy Part" of a Move: The middle of a price movement is considered the "easy" part to trade, as opposed to the uncertain beginning or choppy end.
  • Adjust Positions: Take partial profits on price rises and re-enter on pullbacks.
    • Avoiding the temptation to take profits all together, if the fundamental and technical conditions still point to continued higher prices.
  • Take Profits in Parabolic Markets: If the market accelerates into a parabolic move, consider taking partial or full profits.
    • Reason: Parabolic moves often end abruptly, and waiting too long can result in steep losses if the market reverses quickly
  • Buy high and sell higher: That means buying stocks that have already had good moves and have high relative strength—that is, stocks in demand by other investors.
    • High relative strength (i.e., a stock that is performing significantly stronger than the broad market)
  • I get out when the market action tells me it’s time to get out, rather than based on any consideration of how far the price has gone.

Good Practice

  • Try to be an observer of yourself
    • If instead of saying, “I’m going to do this trade,” you say, “I’m going to watch myself do this trade,” all of a sudden you find that the process is a lot easier. Because then the process of trading focuses on execution of a system
  • Manageable Trade Size: Keep trade size small enough to avoid emotional reactions driven by fear or greed.
    • Reason: Large positions increase psychological pressure, leading to poor decisions.
  • Pre-Position Questioning: Before entering a trade, ask yourself, “Do I really want this position?”
    • Reason: This helps avoid impulsive decisions and ensures alignment with your strategy.
  • Trading Diary: Keep a diary to analyze past trades and learn from mistakes.
    • Reason: Reviewing past decisions offers insights into improving future trading behaviors.
  • Take Breaks: After successful trades, take breaks to avoid mental burnout.
    • Reason: Continuous trading can lead to fatigue and poor decisions; resting prevents this.
  • Adaptability: Stay open to changing opinions or strategies when new information arises.
    • Reason: Flexibility is key to surviving changing market conditions.

Handling Losing Streaks

  • Take a Break: If in a losing streak, liquidate positions and take a break to regain objectivity
    • When I’m wrong three times in a row, I call time out. Then I paper trade for a while.
    • Reason: Continuing to trade during a losing streak can lead to emotional and irrational decisions. A break helps reset focus.
  • Reduce Size: When restarting after a losing streak, trade smaller to rebuild confidence
    • Reason: Smaller trades reduce emotional pressure and allow for clearer decision-making as confidence is restored.
