  • We use the term “object” to refer to any entity that can be named and talked about, that characteristics can be attributed to.

The Artist’s Intention

  • We naturally think about works of art as products of deliberate human activity (Intended by someone)
  • The artist’s intention is a series of psychological states or events in his mind:
    • What he wanted to do
    • How he imagined or projected the work before he began to make it
      • And while he was in the process of making it

Can there be a cause?

  • A good deal of writing about works of art consists in describing the historical situation, the social, economic, and political conditions, understand which they were produced
  • A metaphysical problem: Can a work of art be accounted for as the effect of some set of antecedent conditions?
  • For philosophers who believe in freedom of the will, would argue that there is an element of spontaneity or indeterminism, in the creative act, thus impossible to explain any work of art in any terms/conditions



Evidence of intention

  • The role of intention in evaluating the object
  • The role of intention in describing and interpreting the object
  • Many critics seem to shift back and forth,
    • but if intention and the object are two things (not one thing under two names), then the evidence for the existence and nature of one cannot be exactly the same as the evidence for the existence and nature of other
  • We discover the nature of the object by looking, listening, reading, etc (Internal evidence)
  • We discover the intention by biographical inquiry (letters, diaries, workbooks, interviews…) (External evidence)
  • If there is a conflict between internal and external evidences, we have to decide if we which is the real object

A conflict between internal and external evidences

  • We rely on internal evidences.
  • If there is no internal evidences that support the external evidences, we don’t accept it. (The quality would not occur to us to say they must be there, merely because the artist is convinced that he has put them there)
  • But our perceptions can be influenced by expectation and there may be social stigma for not seeing
  • If the artist has prestige and we are already disposed to regard his intention as a final court of appeal, we would perceive something where otherwise would not
    • If this applies on everyone, for all practical purposes, the quality will be in the object.
    • Thus, it seems the intention, or the announcement of it can not be distinguished from the object

Reject intention as the final court of appeal

  • It’s what we call illusory by ordinary standards.
  • If a quality can be seen in an object only be someone who already believes that it was intended by the artist to be there, then that quality is not in the object at all (Think about a scientist seeing result from an experiment and bias it towards his hypothesis)

More problem with interpreting

  • If the object symbolizes the artist’s intention just by the artist’s intention alone, then we are saying the nature of the object cannot be distinguished from the artist’s intention.
  • Anyone can make anything symbolize anything just by saying it does



The problem of Correct Performance

  • Distinguishing the object itself from the psychological process that produced it becomes more difficult with arts that involve the process of performance between the creator and the perceiver.
    • Bach does not even say what instrument is to perform The Art of Fugue
  • With Classic Music, it is often the conductor’s job to intensify some quality by conducting the music differently.

Plays

  • The art of theatre is ”essentially a “group art” to which the directors, actors, painters, musicians, stage crew and audience all contribute.
  • Not infrequently a new play is half rewritten at rehearsals
    • It’s not what the playwright intended, but what he ought to have intended.



Feature of intentional criticism

  • “Sincere”, “artless”, “spontaneous”, “facile”, “contrived”, “forced”, “subjective”, “personal”, “authentic”
  • Shift reference from art to artist
  • Not clear what the criticism is about

Perceptual (Aesthetic) v.s. Physical

  • Strong atmospheric quality and vividness of color v.s. dark reddish underpaying over the whole canvas
  • To describe the perceptual is to say nothing about how its physical basis was produced

Statement about Aesthetic object v.s. Statement about their effects

  • The painting is cheerful
  • But a painting can not be cheerful, only humans (or animals) can
    • Thus, it’s statement about my response (or the painting’s effect on me)
  • Maybe metaphorically the statement can still be about the aesthetic object

The phenomenal Field

  • Phenomenal objective: self-contained, independent of your will
    • Visual (color, shape), auditory (sound)
  • Phenomenal subjective: something going on in your self
    • Pleasure, anger…
    • Affective terms: any adjective, as applied to a work of art, contains as at least part of its meaning some reference to the effect of the work upon the percipient.




More problem in literature

  • What is it to read a poem or a novel?
  • How can we separate object from response when the experience is all response and no object?

  • In literary works, there are persons, places, and things that happen to them.
  • They are not before you, as reality could be. They are complex and substantial, something you can study, reflect upon, and discover new things about.
  • They exist without your will



The object and its presentations

  • An aesthetic object appears in a phenomenal field as a phenomenal object (presentation, the object as experienced by a particular person on a particular occasion).
  • Is the statement about the object itself or the presentation?

Incompatible representations

  • Two person can have conflicting statements about a work of art.
    • As long as each is content to report his own presentations, there is no contradiction
    • If they both claim to be describing the painting, their claims conflict
  • There is not much use in having the term “aesthetic object“ at all unless we insist upon its freedom from self-contradiction

Can presentation change the object?

  • It seems B is referring to engineering problem of how the building is built, not the perceptual object. Thus B’s statement is aesthetic.
  • That would be too simple.
  • Our knowledge change the presentation’s phenomenally objective characteristics.
    • Should we say the Gothic library really has a dishonest look and A doesn’t have enough to be able to see its dishonesty, or that B’s knowledge is making him see something in the building-as-perceptual-object that is not really there?

Veridical and Illusory Qualities

  • If Schubert’s music sounds pathetic to one who sympathizes with his poverty, that’s a mistake.

  • It’s not a reduction, it only analyzes statements about aesthetic objects into statements about presentations.
  • Where disagreements arise about the characteristics of aesthetic objects they can be verifiably settled by appeal to past or present presentations or to probable predictions about future presentations
